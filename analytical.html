Urban View

Decmber 2023 - Present

Currently, I am dedicating the majority of my time to developing a platform named Urban View. This platform aims to serve as a centralized repository for various open-source datasets, including the Census, ACS, OSM, and other significant spatial datasets. My focus has primarily been on establishing the infrastructure for this project. Once the data infrastructure is completed, I will begin developing novel analysis tools for urban planners. These tools are designed to facilitate calculations of multimodal travel times across entire communities, optimization methods for transportation and housing locations, and, more recently, predictive modeling for community growth.

I have taken a particular interest in Community Scale's (a small urban planning consulting firm) approach to assesing housing needs. Predicting the necessary amount of housing for a community's needs presents a substantial challenge for growing cities and towns. This type of housing analysis seeks to determine what type, where, and at what cost housing is needed for a given community. Ideally, Urban View will enable cross-town and cross-chronology analyses to identify towns similar in time to a studied one and extrapolate the outcomes of these similar towns to the town in question.

Although this project is far from completion, it holds significant value for small towns lacking the resources to attract talent for this work and large cities and consulting firms in need of access to extensive data sets and advanced algorithms.

Senior Honors Project

August 2021 - May 2022

In the period from September 2021 to May 2022, I opted to complete an Honors Project in the Sociology Department, which was awarded the Matilda White Riley Prize for outstanding honors. Initially broad in scope, my project sought to explore innovative methods for modeling and optimizing urban environments to mitigate inequality and segregation. My honors resulted in two components both derived from a digital model of the City of Atlanta constructed from ACS, Census, OSM, and other data public data types.

The first component involved graph partitioning Atlanta's road network to measure the demographic homogeneity of its subgraphs. The second focused on simulating multimodal access to amenities from any given residence to every amenity type within the city. Together, these analyses provide insights into the structural and mobility factors contributing to urban segregation.

Urban Pastures - Graph Partioning

My honors paper explores the methodology and implications of partitioning Atlanta's road network into subgraphs based on travel difficulty, focusing specifically on residential roads. This approach effectively creates isolated pockets of neighborhoods that are only interconnected only through residential streets, creating distinct groups or "Urban Pastures." By excluding all non-residential road segments, the study reveals a pattern of demographic homogeneity within these structurally isolated neighborhoods. The analysis demonstrates a low standard deviation (<1) across key demographic dimensions—race, educational attainment, and poverty levels—within each urban pocket. This finding underscores the extent to which residents with similar demographic characteristics are confined to specific, structurally isolated areas within Atlanta. The term "Urban Pastures" aptly describes these homogenous, structurally determined subgraphs, emphasizing the role of urban infrastructure in segregating communities.

Multi Modal Access Simulation

This component of my Honors was not written up but has broader applications and more significant value for industry. This method was heavy influenced by Spatial Proximity and Connectivity, a paper written by Rice Professor Elizabeth Roberto which developed a nuanced method to measure segregation by thinking about separation measured by distance along the road network. My model adds walk and bike networks to further add nuance to this method. It probabilistically determines likely commute methods from census data and in real time selects the method and determines travel time to all amenities. This model can sample any number of homes in Atlanta and determine the access to amenities or to communities of other demographics. This process could be used as a fitness function for optimization to select the best transit development to offset inequality or identify optimal affordable housing development.
